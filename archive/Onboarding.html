 Why test shape bias in computational models at all?

The shape bias, which is children's tendency to extend novel words by shape rather than by material or texture, has been studied as a window into how children form object categories. The question your project adds is: is this a distinctly human/developmental thing, or does it emerge from exposure to visual statistics?

Vision-language models trained on large amounts of naturalistic image-text data offer a way to ask that question. If a model that has never "developed" in a biological sense also shows a shape bias, that puts pressure on accounts that tie the bias to human-specific learning mechanisms. If it doesn't, or if it's brittle, that's informative in a different direction.

 Why compare models that differ in parameter size and training data?

This is where it gets scientifically interesting. If the shape bias were just a generic property of learning from visual data, you'd expect it to appear uniformly across models. But your project found that models vary along two dimensions: a perceptual preference axis (shape vs. texture tendency) and a task-control axis (whether choices actually track the images or just respond to position). That variance is the data. A small model and a large model both trained on similar data but behaving differently tell you something about what scale, architecture, or training regime is doing. From a cognitive science standpoint, that's analogous to asking: what conditions are necessary and sufficient for the bias to emerge? You're using model variation as an experimental manipulation.

Why does this matter for studying children specifically? The project articulates two reasons.

First, if you want to claim that a model is a useful process model of how children categorize, you need more than a matching score, you need the model to behave coherently when the task is perturbed (counterbalanced, label-stripped, prompt-varied). The validity-gating framework the project builds is exactly that: separating "this model has a shape preference" from "this model is doing something cognitively analogous to what a child does." Second, the stimuli and protocol are designed to be human-compatible, meaning you can run the exact same task with human participants and compare directly.

That bridge from model evaluation to developmental experiment is what makes this useful for a dissertation on children's shape bias, rather than just an ML benchmark.

The no-word control is the clearest theoretical test: if you strip the noun label from the prompt and shape preference drops, that supports the idea that the bias is label-linked (consistent with how it works in children, the shape bias is a bias about word extension, not just shape perception). If it stays stable without the label, that suggests the model is doing something more like a general visual preference, not the linguistically grounded thing children do. That distinction is directly relevant to your dissertation argument.

How to interpret several things in your existing results. What the logit-forced method is and why it matters

Current pipeline: local and remote

  • works by asking the model to generate a response ("1" or"2"). The problem is that generation is noisy: the model can have a positional habit (always saying "1") that has nothing to do with which image it actually matches to the reference. But only at the generational level. That's exactly what the validity gating was designed to catch.
  • The logit-forced approach bypasses generation entirely. Instead of asking the model to speak, you look at the model's internal probability scores for token "1" vs token "2" right at the decision point >> before any generation happens. This is more like a forced-choice read of the model's internal state. (An implicit measure rather an explicit one. Like Reducing the task demands)
  • Combined with swap correction (running each trial twice with shape/texture positions flipped and averaging), it may cancel out positional bias by design, if the bias only appears at the speaking level.
  • The immediate consequence is that SmolVLM was likely not actually uninformative — it was measurement-impaired. After running with logit-forced + swap correction, SmolVLM showed a stable image tracking and shape rate of 40.4% regardless of ordering (order gap = 0%), which is a slight texture bias, not noise. The existing run marked SmolVLM as invalid because of poor image tracking (0.20). But that invalid label was partly an artifact of the generation method, not evidence that SmolVLM has no coherent visual preference. The same logic probably applies to the other invalid models (InternVL, Llama4-scout, qwen3.5-9b, etc.) some of them may have real but previously unreadable signal.
  • This does not invalidate the validity gating framework conceptually. It just flags what level of measurement we are at. It means the interpretation of "invalid" shifts: it was measurement-mode invalid under generation-based prompting, not necessarily evidence of no visual preference at all.
  • The no-word control results also need a caveat. For qwen3.5-9b, the noun-label condition was flagged invalid (image tracking 0.10), yet you ran the no-word contrast and found a large shape drop (−0.326). That delta is hard to trust if the baseline measurement was itself unreliable under generation-mode prompting. The contrast is not meaningless, but the confidence you can place in it as a labeling-effect estimate is lower than for the valid models.
  • The four valid models stand. The validity-gated results for qwen3-vl-4b, qwen3.5-122b-a10b, qwen3.5-27b, and qwen3.5-35b-a3b were obtained under a method that happened to work for those models, they tracked images correctly under generation-based prompting, which means position bias was not dominating. Those results remain interpretable as-is.

  What this means for the next steps

Two decisions to make

  1. Whether to re-run the invalid models with logit-forced + swap correction. This would give you a cleaner estimate of whether those models actually have a shape preference, and would let you make a fairer comparison across all 11 models. The code exists in the levante-bench fork (integrate-shapebias branch). This is scientifically valuable, it turns "6 models failed validity" from a dead end into a dataset.

  2. Whether to migrate fully to levante-bench or keep working in this repo. Mike Frank's position is pragmatic: levante-bench is where the group's shared harnesses (logits API, parsing, model configs) are being developed, so staying in this repo means duplicating maintenance. For dissertation purposes this is mostly a tooling decision, the science (stimuli, task design, analysis pipeline) stays the same. But for the RA onboarding, this is a concrete question: do they set up levante-bench and run shape bias there, or do they port the logit-forced method into this repo?
