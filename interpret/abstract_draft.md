# Abstract (Draft)

Large vision-language models are often evaluated for shape bias using aggregate preference scores, but these scores can conflate perceptual preference with task-response heuristics. We present a reproducible shape-bias benchmark built around a 3-image two-alternative forced choice (2AFC) paradigm in which each trial contains a reference image and two candidates: one matching shape and one matching texture, with candidate order counterbalanced. To support mechanism-level interpretation and future model-human comparison, we engineered and audited a controlled stimulus generation pipeline using 3D object assets, pose-aligned renderings, and texture-consistency checks, yielding reusable stimuli with provenance.

We evaluate local and remote models across families and scales and introduce a validity-gated analysis framework that separates cognitively interpretable behavior from artifacts. Models are first screened for image-tracking under order swaps, lexical sensitivity, and parse reliability; only then is shape bias interpreted as evidence of shape-based categorization. This analysis reveals distinct behavioral regimes, including stable shape-preferring models and models whose apparent preferences are dominated by position/order effects. To test label dependence directly, we run a no-word category control in two stages: a reduced pilot across remote models and a full matched follow-up on a diagnostic trio selected from pilot divergences. The completed trio results show strong no-word shape-bias reductions in some models and near-stable behavior in others, supporting a model-dependent labeling effect rather than a uniform mechanism. We further provide inferential statistics with uncertainty estimates, stimulus-level covariate hooks (for example realism and complexity), robustness diagnostics, and a frozen human-compatible subset with matched protocol.

Our results argue for process-centric evaluation: high shape-choice rate alone is insufficient without behavioral validity. The benchmark and workflow establish a practical bridge from model evaluation to developmental and behavioral human experiments under shared task structure.
